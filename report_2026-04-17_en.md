### 1. Amazon (USA) — Agentic Retail Reinvention + Project Kobe
**Core AI Initiatives:**
- On 9 April 2026, CEO Andy Jassy released his annual shareholder letter, declaring Amazon's intent to rebuild its customer shopping experience from scratch using AI rather than simply adding AI to existing products. Jassy wrote that "the temptation is to just add a little AI to the existing experience," but urged leaders to reimagine experiences "from a clean sheet of paper, assuming you were building with the new technology."
- Amazon disclosed that AWS AI revenue has reached a **$15 billion annual run rate** in Q1 2026 — the first time this figure has been made public. The company's custom chip business (Graviton, Trainium, Nitro) now carries an annual revenue run rate **exceeding $20 billion**, growing at triple-digit rates year-on-year.
- The company confirmed plans to spend approximately **$200 billion in capital expenditure in 2026**, with the majority directed toward AI infrastructure, stating it already holds customer commitments for a substantial portion of the AWS-related capex.
- On Project Kobe (updated 8 April 2026, *Business Insider*): Amazon is developing 225,000-square-foot AI-and-robotics-powered supercenters combining grocery and general merchandise with an embedded automated fulfillment center. Confirmed locations span Orland Park (IL), Cherry Hill and Edison (NJ), and Oak Brook (IL), with internal documents referencing potential for dozens more. Each store is projected to carry ~250,000 SKUs — nearly double a typical Walmart Supercenter. An AI assistant named **Frida** automates category-level inventory decisions; AutoStore robots handle warehouse operations.

**Key Data/Achievements:**
- 2025 full-year revenue: **$717 billion** (+12% YoY); operating income: **$80 billion**
- AI services represent approximately 10% of the total AWS annualized business ($142B run rate)
- Amazon has become the second-largest grocer in the United States, with grocery sales reaching **$150 billion**, driven by a 40× increase in fresh food sales since early 2025
- Project Kobe fulfillment costs are projected ~12% higher than existing sub-same-day networks — a key execution risk

### 2. Walmart (USA) — Agentic AI at Enterprise Scale
**Core AI Initiatives:**
- Walmart's EVP of AI Acceleration, Daniel Danker, declared 2026 the year when "tinkering becomes transformation," accelerating a full-stack agentic AI architecture built around four super-agents for customers, associates, suppliers/sellers, and advertisers.
- The company's **Sparky** AI agent actively handles customer service queries and proactively encourages repeat orders within the Walmart app. Associates use a dedicated back-end AI agent for real-time shelf-restocking prioritization and spill/hazard management.
- Walmart's partnership with **Google Gemini** (announced January 2026) is advancing: products from Walmart and Sam's Club will surface automatically within Gemini conversations via the **Universal Commerce Protocol (UCP)**, enabling discovery-to-checkout without leaving the AI interface.
- Walmart is commercially selling its **Route Optimisation** logistics AI as a SaaS offering, monetizing its proprietary supply-chain technology.
- The company's **Marty** super-agent assists suppliers, sellers, and advertisers; fulfillment centers use AI forecasting to preposition inventory, reroute low-stock items, and optimize perishable delivery routes.

**Key Data/Achievements:**
- AI use has cut fashion production timelines by up to **18 weeks** and improved customer care resolution times by up to **40%**
- US e-commerce sales rose **22% overall**, with the US e-commerce segment turning profitable in Q1 FY2026
- Analyst Jefferies described Walmart as "less of a traditional retail corporation and more of a technology firm"
- Drone delivery expansion: Wing and Walmart extending service to **150 new stores**, reaching >40 million Americans

### Case 1: Amazon — "Clean Sheet" AI Retail Reinvention + Project Kobe Physical AI Integration

**Highlights:**
Amazon CEO Andy Jassy's 9 April 2026 shareholder letter is not a routine investor communication — it is a strategic doctrine. Jassy explicitly rejected incremental AI integration, declaring the company must go "back to the starting line" to rebuild the customer experience from scratch. This philosophy is operationalized simultaneously in two directions: upward, through AWS AI infrastructure ($200B capex, $15B AI run rate), and outward, through Project Kobe — AI-powered physical supercenters designed to make Amazon the dominant force in physical grocery retail.

**Technical Approach:**
- Project Kobe deploys **AutoStore robotic grid systems** (for high-density ambient storage) and the in-house **Orbital system** (covering ambient, chilled, and frozen goods, ~2 years from launch) within each supercenter's ~100,000 sq ft of embedded fulfillment space.
- **AI assistant Frida** automates category managers' inventory decisions, ingesting demand signals and space constraints to generate and validate assortment recommendations — shifting from human-led curation to model-driven selection at scale.
- Amazon's foundational AI forecasting model (deployed across the US, Canada, Mexico, and Brazil) has achieved a **10% improvement in long-term national forecasts** for deal events and a **20% improvement in regional forecasts** for millions of popular items.

**Business Value:**
Amazon holds just 3% of the US grocery market versus Walmart's 21% — Kobe is the vehicle to change that ratio. By embedding fulfillment inside physical retail, Amazon creates a single node for in-store shopping, curbside pickup, and same-day delivery. The critical risk: internal documents acknowledge Kobe fulfillment costs run ~12% higher than existing networks, and the first store is not expected to open until late 2027. This is a long-cycle bet.

**Credibility note:** The Project Kobe details originate from *Business Insider* internal documents (March–April 2026). While unconfirmed by Amazon in full, the company has publicly acknowledged the stores are in "early development." The financial figures from the shareholder letter are primary source disclosures and carry full credibility.

### Case 2: Walmart — Proprietary Agentic AI Infrastructure as Competitive Moat

**Highlights:**
Walmart's AI strategy, analyzed in detail by *Artificial Intelligence News* (13 April 2026), reveals a deliberate choice to build proprietary AI infrastructure rather than rely primarily on third-party platforms. The result is a layered agentic system: Sparky (consumer agent), Marty (supplier/seller agent), associates' restocking agent, and fulfillment forecasting agents — all operating within a proprietary data environment with a $905 billion market cap as validation backstop.

**Technical Approach:**
- Four super-agents coordinate with specialized sub-agents for targeted use cases (e.g., shelf prioritization, perishable routing, supplier ordering)
- Walmart's **digital twin technology** maps new distribution centers before construction; AI forecasting reroutes inventory to low-stock stores in real time
- Route Optimisation AI is now commercialized as **SaaS for external businesses** — a margin-expanding model that turns internal tech spend into revenue
- Integration with Google Gemini via **Universal Commerce Protocol (UCP)**: Walmart retains seller-of-record status and controls fulfillment, while Gemini surfaces relevant products conversationally

**Business Value:**
The UCP integration is strategically vital: Walmart remains the seller of record, protecting its direct customer relationship even as discovery moves to AI interfaces. By commercializing Route Optimisation as SaaS, Walmart begins the transition from pure-play retailer toward a technology company — a thesis endorsed by Jefferies analyst Corey Tarlowe. Fashion production timelines cut by 18 weeks and customer care resolution improved 40% are concrete, quantified wins.

### Case 3: Dollar General + QSIC — AI In-Store Audio as Retail Media Revenue Engine

**Highlights:**
Dollar General's 14 April 2026 announcement of a mass-scale AI audio rollout is one of the week's most actionable in-store digitalization cases. The move is simultaneously a customer experience enhancement, a retail media monetization play, and an AI deployment that reaches communities often bypassed by digital advertising.

**Technical Approach:**
- QSIC integrates **POS transaction data** with curated music and **AI-generated audio ads**, creating hyper-localized, real-time messages that reflect the specific purchasing patterns and community context of each individual store
- The platform exceeds **Interactive Advertising Bureau (IAB) standards** through proprietary ad delivery verification and closed-loop attribution reporting — a key differentiator versus traditional in-store audio
- By Q2 2026, **12,000 stores** (~57% of Dollar General's US footprint) will carry the system

**Business Value:**
This is a concrete example of in-store media monetization at scale, converting physical foot traffic (85% of DG sales) into a measurable, brand-partner-facing revenue stream. The closed-loop reporting — connecting audio ad exposure to POS-verified purchases — transforms in-store audio from ambient noise into an accountable advertising medium. BofA Securities has flagged DG Media Network as a gross-margin lever, and this deployment materially scales that capability.

## d) Insights and Recommendations for IKEA

> *Note: The following recommendations are grounded in this week's developments and are intended to be immediately actionable, not aspirational.*

---

**1. Integrate IKEA's Shopping Experience into Agentic AI Platforms via Open Standards**
Walmart's Google Gemini integration via the Universal Commerce Protocol demonstrates that the next customer discovery touchpoint is inside AI chat interfaces, not branded websites. IKEA should immediately evaluate integration into ChatGPT (following Sephora's March 2026 launch) and Gemini (following Walmart's January 2026 launch). The actionable step: appoint a product lead to evaluate UCP and OpenAI's Instant Checkout API for IKEA product catalog integration, prioritizing high-ticket, high-consideration categories (kitchens, wardrobes, sofas) where conversational guidance most replicates the in-store co-worker advisory model.

**2. Embed Fulfillment Logic into Large-Format Stores (Amazon Project Kobe Learner)**
Amazon's Project Kobe blueprint — dedicating up to half of a large-format store's square footage to automated fulfillment — is directly relevant to IKEA's vast store estates. IKEA should pilot a micro-fulfillment zone within one or two high-traffic stores, using AI-driven demand forecasting to pre-position fast-moving flatpack items for same-day click-and-collect and last-mile delivery. This converts under-utilized back-of-house space into a logistics asset without requiring new standalone facilities.

**3. Deploy AI-Powered In-Store Audio as a Retail Media Revenue Channel**
Dollar General's QSIC deployment — AI-generated, POS-informed audio messaging at 12,000 stores — is a transferable model for IKEA's large-format stores. IKEA's in-store dwell time (average >2 hours) creates significant audio media inventory. The actionable step: issue an RFP to AI audio platform vendors (including QSIC) for a pilot across 5–10 stores, with closed-loop attribution connecting audio exposure to till-verified purchases. Revenue from brand partner advertising partially offsets deployment cost.

**4. Operationalize Conversational AI as a Room-Planning Co-Pilot, Not Just a Chatbot**
Sephora's ChatGPT launch demonstrates that beauty — a consultation-led, personalized, high-consideration category — maps naturally to conversational AI. IKEA's core proposition (room design, lifestyle aspiration, budget-constrained problem-solving) is equally consultation-led. Rather than a generic chatbot, IKEA should build a conversational room-planning agent that ingests room dimensions, style preferences, and budget to generate and modify complete room solutions with purchasable product lists — deployable first within the IKEA app, then as an embedded agent in ChatGPT and Gemini.

**5. Build a Proprietary Data Moat Before Ceding Discovery to Third-Party AI Platforms**
The central risk identified in the Walmart AI analysis (April 13) and in Sephora's BeautyMatter coverage is that AI recommendation engines, not retailer storefronts, will increasingly determine which products are surfaced and purchased. IKEA must accelerate consolidation of its first-party customer data — purchase history, room measurements (from the IKEA app), browsing behavior, loyalty interactions — into a unified AI-ready data platform. This data moat is the prerequisite for both personalization at scale and for ensuring IKEA products rank favorably within third-party AI recommendation logic. The actionable step: audit current data fragmentation across markets and fund a unified customer data platform (CDP) build-out as a board-level priority in 2026.

## e) Risks and Observations

**1. AI Capex Bubble Risk — Conflicting Signals**
Amazon's $200 billion capex commitment has split analyst opinion between "justified infrastructure investment" (Jassy's view, endorsed by customer commitments) and "AI bubble overextension" (investor concern reflected in a >$450B market cap selloff following the February announcement). For IKEA's technology partners and vendors dependent on AI cloud services, this spending cycle creates both opportunity (expanding AI service availability) and risk (potential pricing volatility if the bubble deflates).

**2. Algorithmic Bias and Personalization Governance**
Walmart's own AI strategy analysis (April 13) identifies "preventing algorithmic bias at scale" as a genuine execution risk. As IKEA considers deploying personalization AI across markets spanning vastly different demographic and cultural contexts, governance frameworks for bias auditing, explainability, and local regulatory compliance must be established before deployment, not retrofitted after incidents.

**3. Third-Party AI as Channel Disintermediation Threat**
Sephora's BeautyMatter coverage makes explicit what Walmart's UCP negotiations underscore: as discovery shifts from owned channels to AI interfaces, brand and retailer visibility becomes contingent on algorithmic inclusion, not traffic investment. IKEA's heavy investment in its stores and .com risks reduced returns if new customers are acquired primarily through third-party AI agents. Maintaining "seller of record" status (as Walmart does via UCP) and optimizing product data for AI readability are non-negotiable priorities.

**4. China AI Healthcare Pivot — Competitive Intelligence for IKEA Living**
JD.com and Alibaba's move into AI healthcare retail (April 15) signals that leading e-commerce platforms are aggressively seeking high-retention, high-ticket categories adjacent to their core commerce businesses. IKEA's home wellness and living categories (mattresses, ergonomic furniture, air quality products) may come under increasing pressure from Chinese platform expansion. Monitor JD's "AI + Living Spaces" potential pivot as a leading indicator.
